India FY26: Growth Holds at 7.6% as RBI Cuts 100bps — A Year of Easing Through Global Headwinds

CAUTIOUSLY BULLISH

India navigated FY2025-26 with remarkable macro resilience. Real GDP grew 7.6% (new 2022-23 base year), the fastest among large economies. The Reserve Bank of India delivered 100bps of rate cuts — the most aggressive easing since 2019 — as record-low CPI (0.25% in October) gave the MPC exceptional room to support growth. US Liberation Day tariffs (April 2025) imposed a 26% levy on Indian goods, escalated to 50% in August, before a landmark trade deal in February 2026 brought effective rates down to 18%. Throughout, India's fundamentals held: GST collections hit a ₹2.37 lakh crore all-time high in April 2025 and FY26 total crossed ₹22.27 lakh crore (+8.3%); the fiscal deficit tracked the 4.4% of GDP target; and bank credit growth re-accelerated to 14.3% by February 2026. The combination of good monsoon (106% of LPA), lower oil (Brent ~$58-65 range), rate cuts, and strong government capex execution set India on a solid footing entering FY27.

"The Indian economy continues on a steadily improving trajectory, with real GDP poised to register significantly higher growth of 7.4% in FY25-26. The monetary policy transmission has been working — WALR for fresh loans has declined by 105 basis points in response to the cumulative 125 basis points of repo rate reduction. We are now in a phase of assessment, allowing the rate cuts already delivered to work their way through the system."— RBI Governor

Analysis

RBI cut the repo rate by a cumulative 100bps in FY26 — from 6.25% (inherited from the February 2025 cut) to 5.25% by December 2025. The June 2025 cut was an unusual 50bps (5:1 vote), the largest single cut since 2020. February 2026 MPC held at 5.25%. The MPC was enabled by exceptional CPI conditions: vegetable deflation drove headline CPI to a record low of 0.25% in October 2025, well below the 4% target. Simultaneously, US tariff uncertainty created a downside growth risk that the MPC judged warranted pre-emptive easing. The June 50bps cut reflected urgency — RBI governor Malhotra prioritised growth support over cautious incrementalism.

Outlook

The February 2026 hold and the signalling of a 'prolonged pause' suggest the next 25bps cut will depend on whether CPI stays below 4% into Q1 FY27 (expected 4.0–4.2% per RBI projections) and whether private investment definitively accelerates. Market consensus: one more 25bps cut in H2 FY27, bringing terminal rate to 5.00%.
